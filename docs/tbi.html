Head Injury

The only system here that can leave permanent damage, and only at the last stage. Everything before that heals.

The one thing

The impact is already done. What you manage is everything that follows it. Every point of severity after the initial injury comes from hypoxia, hypotension or CO2, and all three are yours to control.

01

Pressure Inside The Skull

The skull is a closed box. Swelling has nowhere to go, so it raises intracranial pressure, and perfusion is whatever is left over after ICP is subtracted from mean arterial pressure.

That last figure is the one to remember: CO2 raises ICP ten times faster than swelling does. Ventilation is the fastest lever you have on a rising pressure, and it is also the fastest way to make it worse.

Why CO2 moves it so fastCerebral vasodilation

Cerebral vessels respond directly to CO2. Retain it and they dilate, which increases blood volume inside a box that has no spare room, which raises pressure immediately.

Blow it off and they constrict, pressure falls, and this is the mechanism behind hyperventilating a herniating patient. It works. It is also why it is a temporary measure rather than a treatment: constrict the vessels too far and you have traded raised pressure for outright ischaemia.

In practice you aim for 35 to 38 rather than driving it as low as it will go, and letting a head injury hypoventilate is one of the fastest ways to kill them.

02

The Two Secondary Insults

Hypoxia reads oxygen delivery, not saturation. A head injured casualty who is also bleeding accrues damage while their pulse oximeter reads normally, because saturation cannot see that there is nothing left to carry the oxygen.

The conflict with permissive hypotensionNo clean answer

Permissive hypotension is correct for uncontrolled haemorrhage: lower pressure, less bleeding. A head injury wants the opposite, because perfusion is MAP minus ICP and dropping MAP starves the brain directly.

In a casualty with both, these are not reconcilable. You are choosing which one is currently more likely to kill them, and that choice can change over the course of a single casualty.

The practical guidance is that catastrophic haemorrhage wins until it is controlled, because a perfectly perfused brain in a body that has bled out is not a save. Once bleeding is controlled, the priority flips and you want the pressure back up.

03

What Is Reversible

Almost all of it. A brain that has not herniated heals back to zero, and osmotherapy doubles that rate. The exception is herniation, which is stage two only and is the single piece of permanent damage anywhere in this addon.

Everything else is built so a casualty managed correctly, even late, comes back.

Red Flags

  • Letting a head injury hypoventilate. CO2 raises ICP faster than anything else in the model.
  • Hyperventilating for longer than it takes to get them to definitive care. Push it too far and you trade raised pressure for outright ischaemia.
  • Trusting saturation. The hypoxic insult reads delivery, which saturation cannot show you.
  • High PEEP in a head injury. It raises intrathoracic pressure, which impedes venous drainage from the head.
  • Blown pupil with a falling heart rate. Herniation, and the one thing here you cannot take back.
