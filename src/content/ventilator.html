Ventilator

Ventway Sparrow. Turbine driven, so it needs no oxygen to ventilate. Power is what runs out.

The one thing

Every mode guarantees one thing and lets the other float. The danger is always in the one that floats.

01

Ventilation Modes: SIMV, CPAP & IMV

SIMV VC PSVolume guaranteed

You set the volume. The machine delivers exactly that every mandatory breath, at whatever pressure it takes. Synchronised means it waits a fraction for the patient's own effort; pressure support tops up breaths they take in between.

You know precisely what minute ventilation they are getting, which is what you want when you are also controlling CO2 in a head injury.

Volume is guaranteed so pressure is not. If the lung stiffens, the machine forces the set volume in at whatever pressure that now needs. Peak pressure is the number that moves when the patient changes.

SIMV PCPressure guaranteed

You set the pressure. The machine goes to it and holds it, and whatever volume that moves is whatever it moves.

This is the mode for a lung you do not trust: blast, aspiration, contusion. Pressure is capped by definition so you cannot barotraumatise them by accident.

Pressure is safe and volume is not guaranteed. If the lung stiffens further, the same pressure moves less air and they quietly hypoventilate. Watch delivered {{tidal-volume|tidal volume}} and {{etco2|EtCO2}}.

CPAP PS HFNeeds a drive

No set rate and no guaranteed breath. Constant positive pressure splints the airway open and each breath the patient starts gets a boost.

Right for a casualty who is breathing but tiring, or who has an oxygenation problem rather than a ventilation one.

Requires a reliable respiratory drive. On a heavily sedated, opiated or head-injured casualty the machine will sit there supporting breaths they are not taking.

IMV VC (CPR)Sync off

Fixed mandatory breaths, trigger off. It will not synchronise with anything.

Compressions produce pressure changes that look exactly like a patient trying to breathe, so a synchronised mode reads every compression as an effort and stacks breath on breath. Intrathoracic pressure climbs, venous return falls, and the machine makes your compressions worse while trying to help.

Change it back after ROSC.

Battery Endurance & Power

The Sparrow draws ambient air, so it ventilates indefinitely as far as gas goes. Oxygen is optional enrichment up to 95%. The clock that matters is electrical.

Drain is not flat. The turbine works harder against high {{peep|PEEP}}, a fast rate and stiff lungs, so the settings that keep a bad casualty alive are the ones that eat the battery.

05

Reading The Pressure Waveform

One breath, drawn as airway pressure against time. Every number the machine reports is somewhere on this curve, and the two that matter most are separated by the part of it people skip.

Peak, plateau, and the gap between them

Peak pressure is the highest pressure in the circuit, reached while gas is still moving. It contains two things at once: the pressure needed to push gas through the tube and airways, and the pressure needed to distend the lung.

Plateau pressure is what is left once flow stops during the inspiratory pause. Nothing is moving, so the resistive part has dissipated and what remains is the pressure actually stretching the alveoli. Plateau is the number that barotraumatises a lung, not peak.

What movedReadingWhat it means
Peak rises, plateau steadyresistiveSomething in the tube or airway: secretions, a kink, bronchospasm, a bitten tube
Peak and plateau both riseelasticThe lung itself is stiffer: edema, contusion, {{ards|ARDS}}, a pneumothorax, or a chest that cannot expand
Gap widening over timeresistanceWorsening obstruction. Suction and look at the tube before you touch the settings

The two labelled brackets are the same split read off the trace directly. Resistive pressure is the drop from peak to plateau. Elastic pressure is plateau down to baseline, the pressure held against the lung's own recoil.

The four phases

Inspiratory time is flow plus pause. Expiratory time is flow plus pause. Together they are the ventilatory period, and the ratio between them is what you set as {{ie-ratio|I:E}}.

Expiration is passive, which is why that limb is a curve and not a ramp: the lung empties against its own recoil through the resistance of the circuit, fast at first and slower as the pressure gradient falls. Shorten expiratory time past what that curve needs and the next breath starts before the last one finished leaving. The trace never returns to baseline, and the pressure it never returned to is trapped in the chest.

06

Barotrauma, VILI & Gas Trapping

The machine will do what you set it to. If that means forcing a set volume into a lung that cannot take it, the pressure goes wherever it has to go and the lung pays for it. These are the numbers behind that, and they are the reason mode choice is a clinical decision rather than a preference.

The one thing

Volume control guarantees volume and lets pressure run. Pressure is what injures lungs, so on a stiffening lung the setting that keeps ventilation constant is the setting that does the damage.

Where peak pressure comes from

In a volume-targeted breath the machine computes peak pressure from the volume you set and how stiff the lung is. Compliance runs 1.00 for a healthy lung down to a floor of 0.20.

Peak pressure in cmH2O. The gauge turns amber at 30 and barotrauma begins accruing above 35. Read the 500 mL row across: the same setting that is comfortable on a healthy lung is injuring a blast lung.

Pressure control caps this by definition. In PC the pressure is what you set and the volume is whatever that pressure moves, which is the trade the mode exists to make.

The first row is the trap. Each event stiffens the lung, a stiffer lung needs more pressure for the same volume, and more pressure accrues the next dose faster. Left alone in volume control it accelerates.

The pneumothorax it creates is not a cosmetic flag. The addon marks the chest injured and then hands off to ACM's own pneumothorax handler, so it deteriorates and converts to tension exactly like one caused by a fragment.

Stretch, not just pressure

Volume injures independently of pressure. Above 8 mL/kg in a lung already stiffer than 0.80, injury accrues on top of everything above, scaling to full effect by 12 mL/kg. At 70 kg that is 560 mL, which is inside the range people set without thinking.

This is what makes 6 mL/kg an actual treatment in blast lung rather than a slogan, and the machine shows you the number: the live screen reports mL/kg alongside the volume.

Positive pressure on a pneumothorax

An undecompressed pneumothorax is driven toward tension by mean airway pressure, not peak. Mean is PEEP plus about a third of the driving pressure, so PEEP does more damage per cmH2O than the peak does, because it is held for the whole cycle rather than a fraction of it.

There is no threshold on this one. It ramps from a mean of 5 upward, so any positive pressure grows it, and it converts to tension at state 2.5 of 3. Decompression stops it dead: a needle, a thoracostomy or a chest tube all gate it off completely.

Gas trapping and auto-PEEP

The lung needs time to empty, and how much time depends on compliance. If expiratory time is shorter than that, the next breath starts before the last one has finished leaving and the leftover pressure stacks.

Emptying time1.8 s x complianceA stiff lung empties FASTER, so trapping is a soft-lung problem

Auto-PEEP is added to peak pressure, so trapping feeds the barotrauma model directly. It is also shown on the live screen in brackets beside the PEEP you set, which is the only way to tell dialled PEEP from actual end-expiratory pressure.

Clearance is faster than accrual, so lengthening expiratory time recovers quicker than it built. Raise the I:E, drop the rate, or both.

The pressure limit is a valve, not a warning

The limit on ALERTS is enforced. The machine will not push past it, so in a volume-targeted breath a lung needing more pressure than the limit allows simply does not receive the full volume, and the delivered VTe drops below the set VT.

That is the entire point of it, and the trade runs both ways. Set it low and you will not barotraumatise them, but you may not ventilate them either. Set it high and the machine will injure the lung on your behalf without ever refusing.

Red Flags

  • Rising peak with a steady plateau, answered by more volume. The pressure is resistive. More volume through the same obstruction raises pressure further and moves no more gas.
  • Volume control on a lung that is stiffening. The mode holds volume constant by letting pressure climb, and pressure is the thing that injures. Change mode rather than chasing the number.
  • Ignoring the bracketed figure beside PEEP. That is auto-PEEP. It is added to peak pressure and it is feeding barotrauma whether or not you have looked at it.
  • A pneumothorax on positive pressure that has not been decompressed. There is no safe pressure. Mean airway pressure grows it from the moment the machine starts.
  • Setting a high pressure limit to stop the volume alarming. The alarm was the machine telling you the lung could not take the setting.

Red Flags

  • Watching peak and ignoring plateau. Peak includes resistance you may not be able to change. Plateau is the number that tells you what the alveoli are taking.
  • An expiratory limb that does not reach baseline. The breath is being interrupted. Lengthen expiratory time before anything else.
  • Treating a rising peak by turning the volume up. If the cause is resistive, more volume through the same obstruction raises pressure further and moves no more gas.

Red Flags

  • Ventilating a casualty who is empty. Positive pressure opposes venous return. At low volume it can cost a fifth of their stroke volume. Fill them before you tube them.
  • Rising peak pressure in volume control. The lung is stiffening and the machine is forcing through it. Change mode rather than turning up the volume.
  • Falling delivered Vt in pressure control. The same stiffening lung showing itself the other way round, and nothing alarms for it on its own.
  • CPAP on someone without a drive. The machine supports breaths they are not taking.
  • Leaving CPR mode on after ROSC. They now have their own effort and the vent is ignoring it.
