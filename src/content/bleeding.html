Bleeding And Shock

Pressure drives bleeding, and three things decide whether blood clots. Both are modelled, and they pull against each other.

The one thing

Lower pressure means less bleeding. That much holds up, and it is the whole argument for permissive hypotension. The catch is that the same low pressure starves a head injury and drives the acidosis that stops blood clotting at all.

01

Permissive Hypotension

Blood loss is multiplied by how hard the heart is pushing. The reference is a MAP of 70, so at target pressure nothing changes from stock behaviour. Below it bleeding slows, above it bleeding accelerates, clamped between 0.55x and 2.0x.

It self-limits to uncontrolled bleeding only, because ACE already returns zero blood loss for a wound that has been bandaged. Once you have controlled it, the multiplier has nothing to act on.

Clot popPressure reopens wounds

A formed clot is not permanent. Drive the pressure up and it can be blown off, which is the mechanism behind the classic warning about over-resuscitating a casualty who has stopped bleeding.

Chance scales with MAP up to a ceiling of 100, at which point the rate is 1.8 times baseline. Push someone to a textbook blood pressure with uncontrolled bleeding and you will reopen wounds you had already won.

The Lethal Triad

Calcium and citrateThe transfusion trap

Stored blood is anticoagulated with citrate, and citrate works by binding calcium. Transfuse enough of it and you chelate the casualty's own {{ionised-calcium|ionised calcium}}, at 0.18 per litre in the model.

That costs you twice. Calcium is required for the clotting cascade, so it worsens the bleeding you are transfusing for, and low ionised calcium depresses the myocardium, costing up to 40 mmHg of MAP per unit below threshold.

Calcium chloride credits back at 0.12 per gram. This is why calcium sits in current massive transfusion guidance, and the model rewards giving it early rather than after things go wrong.

Acidosis, and how it clearsRecovers faster than it accrues

Two sources. Metabolic acidosis accrues below a MAP of 55 at 0.004 per second and from low oxygen delivery. Respiratory acidosis comes from CO2 retention at 0.006 per second once PaCO2 passes 45.

Both clear faster than they build, at 0.01 and 0.012 per second respectively, so fixing the cause lets the casualty recover instead of carrying a debt they can never pay off.

Acidosis blunts pressor support by up to 45%, which is the in-game version of the real observation that a shocked, acidotic patient stops responding to the drugs that worked ten minutes ago.

Red Flags

  • Chasing a normal blood pressure with uncontrolled bleeding. You will pop clots you had already formed.
  • Transfusing without calcium. Each unit costs ionised calcium, which costs clotting and myocardial function.
  • Treating hypothermia as a comfort problem. Cold blood does not clot, and the effect multiplies with the other two legs.
  • A heart rate that is falling on its own. The compensation is failing, and arrest usually follows.
  • Head injury plus haemorrhage. Permissive hypotension and cerebral perfusion want opposite pressures. Pick deliberately.
