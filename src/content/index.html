01

The Design Rules

Three principles run through every system here. They explain most of the decisions you will find elsewhere in this guide.

Casualties are recoverableNot always evac

The aim throughout is a casualty who can be brought back to being useful rather than stabilised and carried off the map, so almost nothing here creates permanent damage.

The single exception is herniation in the head injury model, which is stage two only. Everything else pays back: a brain that has not herniated heals to zero, acidosis clears several times faster than it accrues, permissive hypotension stops mattering the moment bleeding is controlled, and oxygen delivery is recalculated from scratch every tick rather than accumulating a debt.

The medic sees findings, not numbersHidden physiology

Several systems are deliberately invisible. Oxygen delivery is the clearest case: it is the number that actually decides whether a casualty is dying, and there is no readout for it anywhere in the interface.

Saturation can read 96% while delivery is catastrophic, because saturation is a percentage of the haemoglobin present and says nothing about how much haemoglobin there is. Trusting the number teaches the wrong habit, so you are meant to read the casualty instead.

Being wrong has consequencesNot just being slow

Most medical sims punish hesitation. This one also punishes confident incorrect action, because that is the failure that kills people most often.

A needle decompression on a chest that did not need one creates the pneumothorax it was treating for. Push-dose pressor works less well each time you reach for it. Ventilating a casualty who is empty drops their pressure further. Each of them warns you first, and each is recoverable if you notice.
