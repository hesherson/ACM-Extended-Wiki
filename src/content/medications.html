Reading these

The clinical detail is standard prehospital pharmacology. The Special considerations section on each card is where the modelled behaviour lives: the interactions, the traps, and anything that works differently from stock ACM.

01

Infusion vs Push: Onset & Titration

Anything given as an infusion runs through the roller clamp and ramps in over about 45 seconds, decaying over 120 seconds after the line stops. Pushes act on ACM's own onset curve.

Several drugs are in the vesicant table, where extravasation causes progressive three stage tissue damage over time rather than an immediate injury. That means a bad line is something you find out about later, so check the site before starting and after any move.

Mechanism

Alpha 1 causes {{vasoconstriction|vasoconstriction}} and raises {{svr|systemic vascular resistance}}. {{beta-1|Beta 1}} raises rate and {{contractility|contractility}}. In arrest the alpha effect is what matters, because it raises {{coronary-perfusion|coronary}} and cerebral perfusion pressure during compressions.

Indications

  • Cardiac arrest, any rhythm
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Symptomatic {{bradycardia|bradycardia}} unresponsive to {{atropine|atropine}}
  • Push dose for transient hypotension

Contraindications and cautions

  • No absolute contraindication in arrest
  • Caution in a casualty who is empty, because you are squeezing a tank that has nothing in it

Special considerations

Push dose has tachyphylaxis. Each push returns less than the last, dropping to about a fifth by the sixth dose, and recovering on a {{half-life|half life}} of 420 seconds. The model is telling you the drug is a bridge and the answer is usually blood. Pressor support also ramps over 45 seconds rather than appearing instantly, and decays over 120 seconds after the line stops. Acidosis blunts the whole effect by up to 45%, so correcting pH does more than another dose.

Mechanism

Blocks {{potassium-channel|potassium channels}} and prolongs {{repolarisation|repolarisation}} and the {{refractory-period|refractory period}}. Also has sodium channel, beta and calcium channel activity, which is why it does so many things and has so many side effects.

Indications

  • VF or pulseless VT refractory to defibrillation
  • VT with a pulse
  • Atrial fibrillation with {{rvr|rapid ventricular response}}
  • SVT after {{adenosine|adenosine}}

Contraindications and cautions

  • Torsades de pointes
  • Known {{qt|QT}} prolongation
  • Second or third degree block without pacing

Special considerations

ACM only implements amiodarone for arrest. This addon adds perfusing rhythm control: atrial fibrillation with RVR and atrial {{tachycardia|tachycardia}} convert at an effect of 0.40, SVT at 0.70 because adenosine is first line. Torsades is deliberately excluded, since amiodarone prolongs QT and causes it. A cumulative 2200 mg ceiling models the same concern. Arrest dosing is a 300 mg push; with a pulse it is 150 mg over 10 minutes, and faster than 25 mg per minute causes hypotension.

Indications

  • VF or pulseless VT as an alternative to amiodarone
  • VT with a pulse
  • Local anaesthesia
  • Into an IO before a fluid bolus

Contraindications and cautions

  • Second or third degree block without pacing
  • Known {{amide|amide}} anaesthetic allergy

Special considerations

The most detailed drug model here. A therapeutic dose sits at effect 0.6 and holds full effect for 30 minutes, ramping to zero by 90 minutes. While on board it deterministically terminates VT with a pulse and raises defibrillation conversion odds from a floor of 0.2 to certainty at full effect.

There is also a serum level accumulator with a toxicity arc, because the ACM effect value saturates at 1.0 and cannot represent overdose. {{plateau-level|Plateau}} in mcg/mL is the drip rate in mg per minute divided by {{clearance|clearance}}, so 2 mg/min settles around 3.2, which is therapeutic, and 8 mg/min reaches roughly 12.8, which is the seizure band. Half life is compressed to about 5.5 minutes against a real 100 so a careless drip reaches toxicity inside a scenario. Shock reduces clearance to 60% and esmolol to 70%, which raises the plateau and lengthens the {{half-life|half life}}, so the casualty who most needs the drug clears it worst. Above the seizure threshold an unconscious casualty seizes, and the tell is {{apnea|apnea}}.

Special considerations

This is the correct answer for torsades, which is why amiodarone is excluded from it. An effect of 0.35 terminates active torsades and 0.25 suppresses recurrence, both reachable on a drip. If you are looking at a polymorphic wide complex, reach for this rather than the antiarrhythmic you would use for anything else.

Mechanism

Supplies ionised calcium, which is required for the clotting cascade and for myocardial {{contractility|contractility}}. Chloride salt delivers roughly three times the elemental calcium of gluconate per gram.

Indications

  • Massive transfusion
  • Hyperkalaemia
  • Calcium channel blocker overdose
  • Hypocalcaemia with a low ionised level

Contraindications and cautions

  • Do not run in the same line as bicarbonate or ceftriaxone, it {{precipitate|precipitates}}

Special considerations

Central to the lethal triad model. Stored blood is citrated, and citrate {{chelate|chelates}} calcium at 0.18 per litre transfused. Below an ionised level of 1.0 you lose clotting, worth up to 1.4 times bleeding, and you lose myocardial function, worth up to 40 mmHg of {{map|MAP}} per unit below threshold. Calcium chloride credits back 0.12 per gram. Give it early in a massive transfusion rather than after things go wrong, because you are racing the citrate rather than treating a number. Gluconate is also stocked at 3 g, which is roughly dose equivalent to 1 g of chloride.

Dosing is 1 g after the first unit of blood, then every 3 subsequent units. Doses below 900 mg are ignored outright, so a cautious half dose does nothing at all. Give the gram or do not bother.

Mechanism

Blocks the lysine binding sites on {{plasminogen|plasminogen}}, preventing {{plasmin|plasmin}} from breaking down formed clot. It does not make clot, it stops clot being dismantled.

Indications

  • Haemorrhagic shock within 3 hours of injury
  • Significant traumatic haemorrhage

Contraindications and cautions

  • More than 3 hours from injury, where it increases mortality
  • Known thromboembolic disease

Special considerations

Push it slowly. Given fast it causes hypotension, which is the last thing a bleeding casualty needs. The time window is the teaching point: early it helps and late it harms, so it is a drug that rewards giving it at the point of injury rather than at the aid post.

It halves platelet regeneration while on board. Give it alongside blood products rather than on its own: an antifibrinolytic protects the clot you already have, it does not build one, and slowing platelet recovery in a casualty you are not transfusing costs more than it buys.

  • Induction for intubation, especially in shock
  • Agitation requiring sedation

Special considerations

The addon tracks whether ketamine is on board via fn_ketamineOnBoard, which matters for the sedation and paralysis relationship. A paralysed casualty who is not sedated is a specific failure the model can represent, and ketamine is one of the two agents that answers it. It is the induction agent of choice in a shocked casualty precisely because it does not drop pressure the way propofol does.

The IV analgesia ceiling is 21 mg. Past that you are dosing toward dissociation rather than pain control, which is a different decision with different airway consequences.

Where It Turns On You
Amiodarone into torsades. It prolongs QT, which is the mechanism that causes torsades. Magnesium is the drug that fixes it.
Esmolol running alongside lidocaine. Beta blockade cuts lidocaine clearance to 70 percent, which raises the plateau and pushes a therapeutic drip toward the seizure band.
Lidocaine in shock. Poor perfusion cuts clearance to 60 percent, so the casualty who most needs it is the one who clears it worst.
Ceftriaxone through a calcium line. They precipitate. Massive transfusion means calcium, so flush between or use a second line.
Rocuronium without sedation. Paralysed, fully aware, in pain, and unable to signal any of it.
Naloxone outlasted by its opioid. A casualty who wakes can go back down when the reversal wears off first.
