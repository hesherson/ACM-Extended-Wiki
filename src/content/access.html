PILLAR // SYSTEMS

Access, Infusions
& Blood

Everything that goes into a vein: the catheter, the rate it flows at, every infusion the addon carries with its safe and toxic bands, and the cold chain that decides whether the blood you hang helps or hurts.

The one thing

Bore sets the ceiling, the clamp sets the rate, the bag sets the concentration. The same drug at the same gtt/min is a different dose out of a 100 mL bag than out of a 250 mL bag.

01

Vascular Access & Catheter Gauge

Flow through a cannula follows bore before anything else. Every access type resolves to a multiplier on a base rate of 4.1667 mL/s, which is roughly 250 mL per minute wide open.

AccessMultipliermL/smL/minUse when
14G1.50x6.25375Massive transfusion, you have the vein
16G1.00x4.17250Reference. Standard trauma line

{{io|IO}} FAST10.65x2.71163Sternal, no peripheral access
{{io|IO}} EZ0.55x2.29138Tibial or humeral, no peripheral access

Intraosseous is not the fast option. It is fast to place and slow to infuse, because the marrow cavity resists flow. Both IO devices sit below a 16G peripheral. Reach for IO when you cannot get a vein, not when you need volume quickly into someone who has veins.

IO insertion carries a pain response on fluid bolus onset rather than on insertion, which is when it actually hurts in reality.

02

Roller Clamp & Gravity

The clamp sets an absolute rate in mL/s for its access site, not a fraction of the wide-open gravity rate. It is then capped by the gauge, because no clamp position pushes more through a catheter than its bore allows.

Base rate4.1667 mL/s
Wide open~250 mL/min
Drop factor60 gtt/mL
Hang boostceiling only

Raising the bag raises the hydrostatic column and lifts the ceiling only. A titrated drip running below that ceiling does not speed up, because gravity does not open a clamp. Height helps a wide-open line and does nothing to a throttled one.

04

Blood Products & Cold Chain

Cold blood and warm blood are not the same product. The cooler keeps it viable and the warmer makes it usable, and skipping the second step spends the casualty temperature you cannot easily give back.

StateFlow multiplierCore temperatureClotting
Warmed1.10xNo penaltyUnaffected
Normal1.00xNo penaltyUnaffected
Cold0.65xDropsDegrades via hypothermia leg

Cold product runs at roughly two thirds the rate of warmed, so the transfusion you needed to be fast is the one that is slowest. It also drops core temperature, which feeds the hypothermia leg of the {{lethal-triad|lethal triad}} and degrades clotting, so the product given to stop bleeding is making the blood worse at clotting while it goes in.

The temperature penalty is gated on the cold flag alone. Warmed and normal product carry none.

Blood coolerVirtual container
Inside thaw rate0.20 /min
Display states3

The cooler is a virtual container rather than real cargo, so its contents do not count against carry weight the way loose bags would. Product inside thaws slowly at 0.2 per minute, which is what gives you a working window rather than an instant decision.

Unloading forces the bag into your inventory rather than dropping it, because a bag on the ground is a bag nobody notices going warm.

The fridge is the resupply end of the cold chain. Stock level and regeneration interval are both set at placement, so a mission can run a scarce cold chain or a generous one without a code change.

Y-line transfusionTwo limbs, one patient

A Y-line runs two products into one access site. The practical use is a unit of blood alongside a crystalloid or a warmed line alongside a cold one, without burning a second cannula on a casualty whose veins you have already had trouble with.

Each limb carries its own warmed, cold or normal state, so the flow multiplier and the temperature penalty are resolved per limb rather than for the line as a whole. Running one warmed limb does not rescue a cold one.

05

Citrate & Ionised Calcium

Stored blood is anticoagulated with {{citrate|citrate}}, and citrate works by binding calcium. Transfuse enough fast enough and you strip the recipient of ionised calcium, which is itself a clotting factor. This is the mechanism behind calcium being a non-negotiable adjunct rather than an optional extra.

Citrate load0.18 per litre
CaCl credit0.12 per gram
Calcium floor0.55
MAP costup to 40 mmHg

Below the {{ionised-calcium|ionised calcium}} threshold the clotting penalty reaches 1.4x and up to 40 mmHg of {{map|MAP}} is lost per unit of deficit. A massive transfusion run without calcium makes the patient bleed faster than the transfusion is replacing.

Calcium chloride and calcium gluconate both correct it. Chloride carries roughly three times the elemental calcium per mL, which is why its safe drop rates in the chart above are so much lower for the same effect.

Red Flags

  • Reading a gtt/min without reading the bag size. Same rate, different bag, different dose. It is the most common way to overshoot.
  • Massive transfusion without calcium. Citrate strips 0.18 per litre and the clotting penalty compounds with hypothermia and acidosis.
  • Hanging cold product and calling it done. It runs at 0.65x and actively cools the casualty you are trying to keep clotting.
  • Raising the bag to speed a titrated drip. The hang boost lifts the ceiling only. If the clamp limits, height changes nothing.
  • Treating IO as the fast route. It is roughly half the flow of a 16G peripheral.
