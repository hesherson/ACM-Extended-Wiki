PILLAR // ADVANCED FUNCTIONS

Bleeding
And Clotting

Stock ACE bleeds at a fixed rate per wound. ACM Extended makes that rate answer to blood pressure and to clotting quality, so how you resuscitate changes how fast they empty.

The one thing

Pressure drives loss. Every point of pressure you add to an uncontrolled bleed is pushing blood out of the hole faster. Control the bleeding first and the trade-off disappears.

01

MAP-Scaled Blood Loss Multiplier

ACE behaviour. Blood loss per wound is a fixed rate determined by wound type and bandage state. Blood pressure has no effect on it at all. A casualty at a {{map|MAP}} of 40 and a casualty at 110 lose blood at exactly the same speed.

Override. Loss is multiplied by how hard the heart is pushing, referenced to a MAP of 70 so that target pressure reproduces stock behaviour exactly.

MAPMultiplierMeaning
400.55xFloor. {{permissive-hypotension|Permissive hypotension}} working.
550.75xBelow target, bleeding slowed
701.00xReference. Stock ACE rate.
901.5xOver-resuscitated
1102.00xCeiling

It applies to uncontrolled bleeding only. ACE already returns zero for a bandaged wound, so the multiplier has nothing to act on once you have controlled it. This is the mechanical reason the order of operations matters: tourniquet, then fluid. Reversed, you are pressurising an open circuit.

Why a floor and a ceilingDesign

The curve is clamped at both ends rather than running to zero and to infinity, for two different reasons.

The floor at 0.55x stops permissive hypotension becoming a cheat. A casualty allowed to sit at a MAP of 30 would otherwise stop bleeding almost entirely, which would make deliberate under-resuscitation the dominant strategy and would be wrong: a hole in an artery still leaks at low pressure.

The ceiling at 2.00x keeps an over-resuscitated casualty recoverable. Without it, a medic who pushed too much fluid too early would put the patient into an unrecoverable spiral, and the point of the mechanic is to teach the trade-off, not to hand out unwinnable cases.

What this feels like in playPractical

The visible effect is that a casualty with an uncontrolled junctional bleed who receives a litre of crystalloid deteriorates faster than one who does not, and the medic sees it as blood volume dropping despite fluid going in.

That reads as the fluid not working. It is working exactly as intended: the pressure it bought is being spent pushing blood out of a hole nobody has closed. The correct response is not more fluid.

The same mechanic is why a tourniquet applied late still helps. Once the bleed is controlled the multiplier stops applying to it, and the pressure you have built starts doing what you wanted it to do.

02

Coagulation Multiplier: The Three Legs

ACE behaviour. No {{coagulopathy}} model of any kind. Blood clots identically whatever the casualty's temperature or chemistry, so a cold, acidotic, calcium-depleted patient bandages up exactly like a warm healthy one.

Override. Three multiplying legs feed a single coagulation multiplier, which is read by the same blood loss hook as the pressure term above. They compound, which is the whole point of calling it a triad.

Citrate and calciumThe transfusion cost

Stored blood is anticoagulated with {{citrate|citrate}}, and citrate works by binding calcium. Transfuse enough of it fast enough and you strip the recipient's ionised calcium, which is a clotting factor in its own right.

TermValue
Citrate load per litre transfused0.18
Calcium chloride credit per gram0.12
Ionised calcium floor0.55
MAP lost per unit below thresholdup to 40 mmHg

The consequence is that a massive transfusion without calcium replacement makes the patient bleed worse than the transfusion is helping. This is the mechanism behind calcium being a non-negotiable adjunct rather than an optional extra, and it is measurable in game rather than being a slogan.

Why the legs multiplyDesign

Each leg could have been additive. They multiply because that is what makes the triad behave like a triad rather than like three separate penalties.

A casualty who is only cold is manageable. A casualty who is cold, acidotic and calcium-depleted is not the sum of three problems, they are a patient whose blood has stopped working. Multiplying reproduces that: two mild legs are tolerable, three mild legs are not.

It also means fixing any one leg helps disproportionately, which is the behaviour you want to teach. Rewarming a cold casualty pays back more than the temperature change alone suggests, because it divides out of the other two.

03

Install Point & Verifying It Is Live

Both terms are read inside ace_medical_status_fnc_getBloodVolumeChange, replaced at compile time through ACMI_overwrite_medical_status. That function is not compiled final by ACM, so the replacement installs cleanly.

The replacement sets ACMI_infusion_getBloodVolumeChangePatched on the unit each time it runs. If you are debugging and want to confirm the override is actually live rather than assuming it, read that variable: it holds the mission time of the last run.

Red Flags

  • Fluid before haemorrhage control. Every mmHg you add multiplies the loss through an open hole. Stop the bleeding first.
  • Massive transfusion without calcium. Citrate strips ionised calcium at 0.18 per litre and the clotting penalty compounds with the other two legs.
  • Treating a cold casualty as a temperature problem only. Hypothermia is a clotting problem that happens to show as a number on a thermometer.
