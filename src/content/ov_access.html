PILLAR // ADVANCED FUNCTIONS

Access
And Flow

What actually determines how fast fluid goes in: catheter bore, the height you hold the bag, and the roller clamp. Stock ACM treats most of this as one number.

The one thing

The clamp sets an absolute rate, not a fraction. Gravity flow is roughly 250 mL per minute. A titrated drip is a small number of mL per minute. Treating the clamp as a percentage of gravity made every infusion run about ten times too fast.

01

Catheter Bore

ACM behaviour. A flow table keyed by access type, with a base rate of 4.1667 mL per second inherited from ACE's medical engine. ACM models 16G, 14G, and two intraosseous devices.

Override. The table is reproduced and an 18G catheter is added. An 18G is a smaller bore than a 16G and flows correspondingly slower, which matters because 18G is the realistic field choice in a lot of situations where a 16G is not achievable.

The 18G factor is exposed as ACMI_iv18gFlowFactor and defaults to 0.55, so it can be tuned without touching the table.

Why intraosseous is slower than it looksPractical

Both IO devices sit below a 16G peripheral line, at 0.65x and 0.55x. That surprises people who think of IO as the fast option because it is quick to establish.

Quick to place and quick to infuse are different things. The marrow cavity offers real resistance, which is why IO frequently needs a pressure bag or a push-pull technique to move volume at a useful rate.

The practical consequence in game is that IO is the right answer when you cannot get a vein, not when you need volume fast into someone who has veins available.

02

The Roller Clamp

Override. The clamp publishes an absolute flow rate in mL per second for its access site, clamped to the gauge's physical maximum. It is not a fraction of the gravity rate.

The bug this fixedTen times too fast

Treating the clamp as a fraction of the wide-open gravity rate meant that dialling in a slow drip still produced a fast infusion, because a small fraction of 250 mL per minute is still a lot of fluid.

The symptom was bags emptying at roughly ten times the dialled rate, which made titration meaningless: any drug you were trying to run slowly went in as a bolus.

With an absolute rate the bag drains at exactly the drops per minute you set. The gauge maximum still applies as a ceiling, because you cannot push more through a catheter than its bore allows however far you open the clamp.

When no tracked infusion is present the rate is published as negative, which the flow function reads as wide open.

Holding the bag upHydrostatic column

Raising the bag raises the hydrostatic column, which raises flow. While a medic holds a bag aloft, ACMI_hang_flowMult is set on the patient and defaults to 1, meaning no change.

The important detail is that the hang boost raises the ceiling only. A titrated drip running below the cap is unaffected, because gravity does not make a clamp open further. Only a wide-open line benefits.

That is the correct behaviour and it is also the one people find counterintuitive: holding the bag higher does nothing at all if the clamp is what is limiting you.

03

Where This Is Installed

ACM_circulation_fnc_getIVFlowRate is compiled final by ACM, so an earlier runtime reassignment of it was silently ignored and had no effect at all. It is now replaced at compile time through ACMI_overwrite_circulation.

The file is a full de-macroed replacement of ACM's flow table. If ACM changes its gauge multipliers, re-pull the source rather than editing our copy to match.

Red Flags

  • Expecting IO to move volume like a large-bore peripheral. It flows at roughly half, and needs pressure to do better.
  • Raising the bag to speed up a titrated drip. The hang boost only lifts the ceiling. If the clamp is limiting, height changes nothing.
